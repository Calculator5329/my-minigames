# Hotel Cascadia — canonical plan

Plan — 2026-04-19
Status: **story locked.** Implementation pending.

This document supersedes both `2026-04-19-switchboard-design.md` (original
'418 Linden' design) and `2026-04-19-switchboard-redesign.md` (the
dissociative-house rewrite). The game keeps its `id` of `switchboard`
(saves and asset paths stay valid) but renames in the selector card to
**Hotel Cascadia**. The operator-board mechanic is the only surviving
constraint from prior plans. Story, cast, dialogue, voices, endings, and
Night 5 are fully replaced.

## STORY (locked — do not rewrite without sign-off)

### One-line

You are the night switchboard operator at Hotel Cascadia. The building
is rebuilding itself around you. So is everyone in it. So are you.

### The four paragraphs

> **I.** Hotel Cascadia opened in 1934 on a hillside that does not appear
> on any survey map, and it has not stopped opening. The architect, a
> man named Auber Quint, died on the third floor of a stroke before the
> ribbon was cut. He had been so angry about a billing dispute with the
> general contractor that the anger outlived him in the framing. The
> hotel kept building. It built a fourth floor over his coat, still
> folded on the back of a chair. It built a fifth floor over the
> drafting table. By 1938 it had a sixty-second floor, then a hundredth,
> then a four-hundred-and-eighth, and the ribbon was never cut because
> there was no longer a single front to cut a ribbon across. Carpenters
> who came up the hillside to look for work in the morning came back
> down at night not remembering they had been hired. The hotel hired
> them again the next morning. It is still hiring.
>
> **II.** Guests check in. The hotel does not let them check out. It
> instead checks them *in again*, a little younger, a little less sure
> of the year, and gives them a different room. Mrs. Kestral in 412 has
> been calling the desk about a leaking faucet since 1962; her room has
> been upside-down for forty of those years and she has been standing on
> the ceiling for forty of those years and she has only just realized.
> Mr. Ashworth in 88 was here on a sales conference; he has been here on
> a sales conference for eleven years and there are now seven other men
> in his room who are also Mr. Ashworth. The mirror in 1102 has stopped
> reflecting Dr. Pryce, and Dr. Pryce has noticed, and Dr. Pryce has
> begun calling the front desk to ask, very politely, whether the
> operator has noticed yet what is happening to her. Some guests have
> begun figuring it out before the hotel resets them — they ask why
> their room is upside-down, why there are so many invisible people
> standing in the corner staring, why the stairs only descend, why the
> stairs descend forever and never connect to a floor. They are reset
> by morning. Sometimes a reset takes wrong: a sixty-year-old woman
> wakes up at nine years old in room 312, with her hair the wrong
> color, mouthing "Mama" into a tin telephone that is not plugged into
> anything.
>
> **III.** You sit in a small office on what the brass plate calls
> Floor Zero. There is no clock in the office because the architect's
> grievance against the contractor included a clock he refused to pay
> for, and the absence of that clock spread through the building. Time
> does not move at Hotel Cascadia. Days do not pass. Sunrise is a
> rumor the weatherman keeps reading on the in-house radio. Your
> switchboard is built into the desk, brass and bakelite, and it grows
> a new line every time the hotel grows a floor. You came in for the
> overnight shift on the seventeenth of October, 1956. You are still
> on the overnight shift. The lobby outside your office is the same
> lobby. The bellhop standing by the elevator is the same bellhop.
> Some of the rooms behind the wallpaper of your office are full of
> previous shifts of operator. They are the soft thumping you have
> learned not to hear. You will become one of them on Night Four,
> when the Replacement calls in from your own office to ask whether
> tonight is her first night, because it is, because the hotel has
> already hired her, because you are already gone and just don't know
> it yet.
>
> **IV.** There is one door behind your desk that the brass plate
> calls SUPPLY. You have opened it three times in thirty years and
> every time it was a closet with a mop. On Night Five you will open
> it again and it will not be a closet. It will be a hallway you have
> never noticed in thirty years of looking. At the end of the hallway
> is a door, and behind the door is a street, and over the street is
> a sky. You will not recognize the sky. It will be the wrong year by
> a wide margin. You will sit down on a bench. The bench will be
> cold. You will close your eyes for one moment because the sun is so
> bright and you have not seen the sun since 1956. When you open them,
> the line will be ringing. You will be at the desk. The hotel will
> have four thousand two hundred floors now. There will be a new lamp
> on your board you have never seen before, and the brass plate above
> it will say ROOFTOP, and the door behind you will say SUPPLY again,
> and the closet will be a closet again, with a mop. The hotel always
> wins. The hotel is patient because the hotel is the patient.

## Mechanics (locked)

The board mechanic is preserved. Everything else is layered on top.

### M1 — The board grows
Night 1 starts with a 6-line board (existing UI accommodates this with
no layout change). Night 2 has 8. Night 3 has 10. Night 4 has 12. Night
5 has 14. Each new line that appears after Night 1 is announced by a
single hammer-strike sound effect during the inter-night Operator's Log
card and a small "NEW LINE INSTALLED" stamp on the directory entry. The
hotel is building you a new line.

### M2 — The 3:14 AM event
Each night the in-game clock crosses 3:14 AM exactly once. For three
in-game minutes, all currently-lit lamps go dark and a single new lamp
lights with no caller name. If the operator picks it up, the line
plays only inhalation (the architect). Routing it to **line 3** during
the window — the original third floor — counts as "let him rest" for
that night. Any other route, or letting the window expire with the
call unanswered, *adds* time to the corridor walk on Night 5 (each
miss = +1 corridor length unit; eight or more makes the corridor
infinite, which forces the UNDERSTUDY ending).

### M3 — Dead-socket bellhop
Lines 11 and higher (which appear N3+) sometimes ring with no caller
name and no sound. If the operator answers, only breathing (the
bellhop, post-shaft). Routing these calls to *anywhere* registers as a
miss. The intended play is to recognize them and let them ring out.
The dead-socket bellhop is the player's first hint that "do not pick
this up" is a valid action.

### M4 — ARCHIVED stamp
Once per night, a directory entry briefly shows the word **ARCHIVED**
in red over a guest's name (Mrs. Kestral, then Mr. Ashworth, then Dr.
Pryce on subsequent nights). Calls to that guest still route. The
stamp lingers for 4 seconds and then fades. The stamp is a tell that
the hotel has already reset that guest at least once. No mechanical
effect; pure dread tell.

### M5 — Ledger (replaces the inter-night Operator's Log)
After each night, the operator writes in the ledger. The card is the
same UX as the prior plan's "Operator's Log," but the writing is the
hotel's, not the operator's. Each ledger card includes:
- A floor count line: `FLOORS THIS MORNING: 412` (rises each night).
- A guest count line: `GUESTS IN INVENTORY: 1,114`.
- One unsigned note: e.g. *"Room 412 reports a leak. Mrs. Kestral has
  reported a leak every morning since the building was built."*
- The N4 ledger always includes: *"A new operator was hired tonight.
  She has begun her first shift in your office."*

### M6 — Painted window
Behind the operator's desk there is a window (`board.js` background
layer). It is painted black on the outside. On Night 3 the paint
begins to flake. By Night 5, through the flake, the player can see a
sliver of a corridor that is not anywhere in the hotel. (Pure visual.)

### M7 — Wallpaper
The operator's office has wallpaper. On Nights 1–2 it is striped. On
Night 3 a single panel sags. On Night 4 a smear of dried red runs from
behind the panel down to the baseboard, and a glitched guest's writing
is visible through the gap: **"THE STAIRS GO DOWN FOREVER"**. Pure
visual; 0.5s subliminal flash on N4 entry, persistent on N5.

### M8 — Reset risk for the operator
Composure replaces the prior "score" framing. Composure is the
operator's grip on which year it is. Each missed call, wrong route, or
accumulated ringing line drains composure as it does today. **New:**
when composure drops below 25, a new effect: the on-screen clock above
the board flickers from `1986` to `1973` to `1956` and back. The board
header brass plate flickers between `FLOOR ZERO` and `FLOOR ZERO ‑‑
NIGHT 1` — the hotel is starting to reset you. If composure breaks,
instead of "Game Over," the screen fades and reopens at the start of
the same night with a new, polite young woman's voice on a tutorial
overlay: *"Welcome to your first shift at Hotel Cascadia."* You are
the Replacement now. (Mechanically: failure replays the night with no
penalty; thematically: that's what just happened to you.)

### M9 — The Replacement on Night 4
Beginning Night 4, one critical call (`critical: true`) comes in from
the **Replacement** at exactly 3:14 AM (overlaps the architect window).
The Replacement is calling from the operator's office. Her caller card
shows the room as **FLOOR ZERO**, the same as the operator's. The
player has three valid actions and they bind the ending:

1. **Connect Replacement → FLOOR ZERO** (her own room — the operator's
   room). This is the loop. Score the call as a hit. Bind ending CHECK
   OUT.
2. **Deny Replacement (D key)** or let her ring out. Bind ending
   UNDERSTUDY.
3. **Connect Replacement → 3rd floor (line 3, the architect's
   line).** Sets a third bind that contributes to ending DEMOLITION
   eligibility, *if* the architect was also let-rest correctly across
   N1–N4 (M2 conditions met).

Failing to act before the 3:14 window closes counts as deny.

### M10 — The supply-closet door (N5 only)
Night 5 replaces the prior 'walkthrough through the house' with a
walkthrough through the operator's office. The same `walkthrough.js`
top-down view is reused. The room is small: desk, board, mop closet
(brass plate: SUPPLY), painted-over window, wallpaper. Mrs. Kestral's
room is upside-down on the floor below; the player can see her feet
through a hole in the floorboards. The bellhop stands in the doorway,
silent, breathing. The Replacement sits at the desk; if she was
connected on N4 she is *the operator now* and the player is a guest
walking the room she works in. If she was denied she is not there at
all and the desk is dusty. If she was routed to line 3 she sits at the
desk silent and the architect's coat is folded on the chair next to
her.

The interaction binding:

- SPACE on the bellhop → he inhales. Nothing else.
- SPACE on the desk → reads the ledger out loud. Last entry says the
  operator's name has been left blank since 1956.
- SPACE on the wallpaper sag → reveals **"THE STAIRS GO DOWN
  FOREVER"** in dried red. Composure −15.
- SPACE on the painted window → crack widens. Sliver of corridor.
- SPACE on the SUPPLY door → opens the door. **This is the only way
  to end the night.** Branches by ending bind from M9 + M2:
  - **CHECK OUT** (default loop): see "Endings" below.
  - **UNDERSTUDY**: door opens onto a hallway that extends as the
    player walks. Player walks for a long time. Ends with a board they
    have never seen before, lit lamp.
  - **DEMOLITION**: door opens onto the third-floor mezzanine. Coat
    folded on the chair. Architect's bones are visible in the wall
    framing. SPACE on the bones → operator unburies him. Hotel folds
    in. Operator walks out.

## Endings (locked)

### A — CHECK OUT *(default loop, the "good" ending)*

**How earned:** On N4, Replacement was connected to FLOOR ZERO
(line 0/your line). Architect window may have gone any way.

**Plays out:**

> The door opens onto a hallway you have never noticed in thirty years
> of looking.
> You walk to the end.
> A second door.
> Sunlight on the other side.
> You step out onto a sidewalk.
>
> The street is loud. There are cars you do not recognize. A man walks
> past you wearing a thin black coat and a small black device pressed
> to his ear. He is talking to no one and to everyone. He does not see
> you.
> A woman crosses the street with a child. The child is holding a
> small flat black thing and saying *"Mama, I'll be home in an hour"*
> into it. The child's voice is your voice. The mother does not see
> you either.
>
> You sit down on a bench. The bench is cold. The sun is brighter than
> you remember the sun being. You hold up your hand and look at it.
> Your hand has the same lines on it that it had on the seventeenth of
> October, nineteen fifty-six.
>
> The year above the bank across the street says **2026.**
>
> You did not lose thirty years. You lost seventy.
>
> Your eyes are heavy.
> The sun is warm.
> You close your eyes for one moment.

— *fade —*

> A lamp is lit on line 1.
> The clock above the board says 9:02 PM.
> The brass plate on the door behind your desk says SUPPLY.
> The closet inside it is a closet. There is a mop.
> The board has four thousand two hundred lines now.
> A new lamp on the top row is lit. The brass plate above it says
> ROOFTOP.
> A polite young woman's voice on the tutorial overlay:
> *"Welcome to your first shift at Hotel Cascadia."*

**End screen:** `YOU CHECKED OUT.` Score: 300.

### B — UNDERSTUDY *(you become the next operator's hallway)*

**How earned:** On N4, Replacement was denied (D key, ringout, or
misroute to anywhere not FLOOR ZERO and not line 3).

**Plays out:**

> The door opens onto a hallway you have never noticed in thirty years
> of looking.
> You walk to the end.
> The hallway has not ended.
> You walk further.
> The hallway has still not ended.
> You walk for what feels like a year.
> The lights dim.
> The wallpaper changes pattern.
> You become aware that you have not breathed in some time and that
> something else is breathing for you.
> At the end of the hallway is a switchboard you have never seen
> before.
> A lamp is lit.
> You sit down.

**End screen:** `YOU TOOK THE NEXT SHIFT.` Score: 120.

### C — DEMOLITION *(the only real out)*

**How earned:** Across N1–N4, the architect's 3:14 window was answered
correctly (routed to line 3) **at least three of four nights**, AND on
N4 the Replacement was routed to line 3 (handed off to the architect
rather than installed at your desk).

**Plays out:**

> The door opens onto the third floor.
> The mezzanine is empty.
> A coat is folded on the back of a chair. The coat is pinstriped.
> The pocket watch on the chair has stopped at 3:14.
> The drafting table is set with an unbuilt floor plan for the second
> floor.
> Behind the wallpaper the bones of a man are visible in the framing.
>
> You unwind the wallpaper.
> You take the bones out of the wall.
> You lay him on the drafting table.
> You fold his hands.
> You take the coat off the chair and lay it over him.
>
> The breathing in the shaft stops.
> The lights flicker once.
> The bellhop in the elevator opens his eyes for the first time in
> ninety-two years and walks out of the building.
>
> The hotel folds in on itself. Floor 4,201 falls into floor 4,200,
> which falls into floor 4,199. It takes three minutes. The carpenters
> on the hill stop work and go home, this time remembering that they
> were hired.
>
> You walk out the front door.
> The street is the street again.
> The sky is the sky again.
> Behind you there is a parking lot, with a chain-link fence around it,
> and the year on the bank across the street says nineteen seventy-one.
> You have lost fifteen years. You will get the rest of your life back.

**End screen:** `YOU LET HIM REST.` Score: 600.

### Failure (composure ≤ 0)

Replaces the prior `gameOver()` flash. The fade-in copy:

> The lamps are too loud now.
> You stop hearing them. You stop hearing yourself.
> The board rings, and rings, and rings.
> A polite young woman's voice on the tutorial overlay:
> *"Welcome to your first shift at Hotel Cascadia."*

The night restarts from its first call. No save advancement. The
operator is now the Replacement. (Same mechanic as Reset Risk in M8.)

## Implementation order (proposed)

1. **Doc + manifest pass** *(this commit).* Lock the doc, rename the
   selector card title to `Hotel Cascadia`, no code behavior change.
2. **Content rewrite.** Rewrite `content.js` end-to-end (voice profiles,
   directories, all night scripts, walkthrough rooms, endings). No
   audio yet — game runs on SpeechSynthesis fallback for one
   commit so behavior can be validated without a 38MB asset diff in
   the same change.
3. **Engine wiring.** `nights.js`, `board.js`, `walkthrough.js`,
   `game.js`, `voices.js` — all the new mechanics (M1–M10), the
   ledger card, the ending bind, the Replacement-takeover restart.
4. **Audio re-bake.** Delete retired assets; run
   `scripts/generate-voices.js`; commit the new wav/txt set.
5. **Polish + playtest.** Validate pacing curve N1→N4, validate the
   three endings, validate the failure-restart-as-Replacement loop.
6. **Changelog + roadmap update.**

Each step is a self-contained commit so a regression in audio doesn't
also regress engine, and so the doc/manifest rename is reviewable in
isolation.
